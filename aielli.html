🌿 Village Diaries  ·  №3

Aielli

Resilience Written on Walls

Marsica Region Abruzzo July 2026
← Back to Greenwingo

There are places we visit for a long time before we truly begin to see them. I used to come to Aielli years ago, for a Saturday night pizza. It was a lighthearted ritual, a carefree moment shared with family. In those years, I wasn’t looking at the village; I was looking at the life I had ahead of me, and the town was just the faded backdrop of an ordinary evening.

I returned recently, on a regular weekday, with a different intention. I deliberately chose the silence: no festival, no event, no crowds of tourists filling the alleys. I wanted to strip this place of its festive attire to find its essence, to understand what remains of a village when the spotlights go out and only its ordinary rhythm is left.

Aielli welcomes you from its altitude of 1,020 meters, clinging to a rocky spur that dominates the Fucino plain. From up here, geography becomes emotion: on one side, your gaze is lost in the immense basin that once held a lake and is now a checkerboard of farmland stretching to the horizon; on the other side is Celano, with its severe mountains and the valley cutting sharply between them.

The transition begins during the climb.On a clear day, the ascent is accompanied by the intense scent of fresh vegetation, a smell that drifts through the windows before the village even comes into view. Then, all of a sudden, the first murals appear. They are bold, immediate splashes of color. It is the calling card of a community that decided to respond to depopulation with a visual revolution.

Leaving the car in the main square, where the horizon opens up generously and locals walk unhurriedly, we head toward the highest point. The alleyways are quiet, inhabited by small, familiar sounds: isolated footsteps, children running, and the clattering of a few open construction sites. There are houses being renovated and scaffolding busy healing the wounds left by time. It is a sign of care: the village is not letting itself go; it is healing itself. We reach the belvedere, where a shaded bench feels like an explicit invitation to stop and contemplate the majesty of the valley. Nearby, stone turns into literature: Dante's entire Divina Commedia has been transcribed onto a monumental wall. Close by are a few curious, harmonious art installations made of metal and recycled materials, which seem to hold a dialogue with the surrounding architecture.

The climb culminates at the Torre delle Stelle (Tower of the Stars). Built in 1356 by Ruggero, Count of Celano, it served its military function for centuries, scanning the ground below to defend against enemies on the plain. In 2002, however, this stone cylinder completely flipped its perspective: transformed into an astronomical observatory and planetarium, today it no longer looks down at the earth, but points straight toward the infinite. Finding it closed, I realize it doesn't matter. Many of the surrounding murals revolve around this very concept: space, constellations, the human quest. It is as if the presence of the Tower gave the entire village permission to lift its eyes to the sky.

This stone cylinder completely flipped its perspective: today it no longer looks down at the earth, but points straight toward the infinite.

Right beneath the tower, another monumental wall hosts the complete text of Ignazio Silone’s novel, Fontamara, written word for word. Set right in this peasant land, the novel seems to inhabit that wall completely naturally.I chose to be a pure spectator, letting the words speak to me on their own.

Words of Fontamara, anchoring the village's identity in Marsican resilience.

Descending the steps, we immerse ourselves in authentic life. Balconies are filled with flowers and the alleys narrow around homes. The murals blend into everyday life; some evoke peace, while others play with the warm colors of space.

I stop to observe two adjacent houses: one worn by time, peeling and ancient; the other perfectly restored and decorated. Here, the present does not erase the past, and contemporary art is not a trick to hide the old age of the walls. It is a patina that adorns them.

I enter a small gallery and find myself in front of the facade of the Chiesa della Santissima Trinità, an ancient church dating back to the fourteenth century. Inside, a Mass is being celebrated. The priest talks about emptiness of the soul, deep loneliness affecting those without inner word to follow.

The quiet, welcoming presence of the ancient Chiesa della Santissima Trinità.

We are used to thinking of wisdom as heritage of old age. But in our societies, it is precisely the young who are afraid. Suffer not for lack of spirituality, but from absence of a guide—a beacon to show them a path and offer a perspective of optimism for the future.

Finding deep reflections where you least expect them.

As I step out of the church, the village reclaims its collective breath: young people gather for aperitivo, elderly sit outside their front doors chatting about everything. Here, alienating loneliness does not exist. The past remains the solid foundation upon which the future rests.

Where nature and community stubbornly remain.

I left carrying the same pizza from back then. But this time, in the rearview mirror, I left behind a place that I had finally looked straight in the eye.
